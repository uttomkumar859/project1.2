So you’ve decided to go the Data Scientist Bootcamp route. Good for you! But what comes next? There are so many bootcamps to choose from. How do you find the best fit for you? Keep these simple tips in mind, and you can’t go wrong:

  • Create Your Career Goals

    Do you have a timeline? Are you looking to start a data science career immediately after you finish the bootcamp? Where do you want your career to be several years from now?

  • Figure out Your Budget

    Bootcamp prices vary, so you need to take stock of your finances and see what you can afford. Does the bootcamp offer financing options? And if so, are you ready to take on more debt?

  • Figure Out Your Schedule

    Are you working full-time? Are you going to have enough time and energy after a long day of work to devote to your new studies? Are there any non-work obligations that may prove to be an obstacle?

  • Figure Out Yourself

    Do you like getting taught in a slow, measured, gradual fashion, or do you prefer undergoing intense training for a shorter period, thereby getting it over with sooner?

  • Find Out Where the Bootcamp is Held

    If you’re going to attend the bootcamp in-person, it’s probably a good idea to make sure it’s somewhere close by. Many of the larger learning institutions hold bootcamps in multiple cities. Is one close to you?

  • Decide What Aspects of Data Science Appeal to you the Most, and Shop Around

    Not all bootcamps operate the same way. For instance, some give more time to programming languages, while others barely touch the subject. Check out different curricula and see which ones give more attention to the things you especially want to know.

  • Read Testimonials and Other Online Resources

    Many bootcamp websites have recommendations and testimonials from previous attendees. You can also check out entries on Reddit or Quora.

  • Check Out the Prerequisites

    Maybe the online bootcamp you want has specific requirements you must meet before signing up.

There are as many online IT-related bootcamps as professionals across disciplines need to stay up to date with today’s rapidly changing technologies. Bootcamps provide a new way to learn IT-related skills, and are offered in a more short-term, immersive experience than traditional educational settings. Ultimately, the bootcamps teach hopeful IT professionals the skills they require for a career in their preferred field.

The typical bootcamp lasts between six weeks to 24 months, although most programs run from 12 and 40 weeks. Bootcamps deliver intensive learning coupled with great speed—after all, there is a lot of information to absorb, and time is limited! As a result, bootcamps limit instruction to the data science skillsthat are most relevant and needed for the position that the student is interested in getting. You don’t go to a programming bootcamp expecting to learn how to do cloud-based backups, for instance.

The better bootcamps provide an intense, accelerated learning experience coupled with hands-on data science projects that let students practice applying their newfound skills and knowledge. Also, they help their graduates find jobs in their chosen field—a huge advantage in super competitive areas.

You can find all kinds of bootcamps. Since the hallmark of a good bootcamp is flexibility and accessibility for students, they are broken down into two main types, each specially suited to accommodate a different student situation:

  • Part-Time Bootcamps

    Part-time bootcamps run approximately 30 weeks (give or take) and are ideal for people with busy schedules—which usually means that they have a job. These camps typically require a weekly commitment of about 20 hours, done via the internet or a physical location, depending on the bootcamp. Part-time bootcamps also pose less of a strain on the student’s financial resources. Although this camp doesn’t ask for much time and money, it takes longer to complete. There had to be a catch, right?

  • Full-Time Bootcamps

    If you want an intense learning experience, look no further. Full-time bootcamps typically ask for a commitment of up to 80 hours per week over a two to seven-month period, averaging approximately 17 weeks. Full-time camps offer a fully immersive learning experience, and come with a correspondingly higher cost. Luckily, if you’re taking classes to help you perform better at your job, your company may absorb some or even the entire cost. But be warned: if you decide on a full-time bootcamp, then that’s your life for the camp’s duration. Don’t plan on working a full-time job simultaneously.

Bear in mind that the time figures aren’t set in stone, nor are they the same for every bootcamp offered. For instance, one specific bootcamp offers part-time at six hours per week for 10-12 weeks, and full-time at 40 hours a week for 10-12 weeks.

Besides the full-time/part-time dichotomy, there are the questions of where you attend bootcamp and how you participate in it. Your choices are:

  • In-Person

    You physically attend the coding bootcamp. You take organized, structured classes at a given location. You have access to a teacher to assist you if things get rough. If you prefer structure and keeping your academic life separate from the rest of your life, this is the best choice, assuming you have the time to participate. Examples of in-person camps include, Betamore, Data Engineering Bootcampand The Data Incubator.

  • Online

    If you would rather learn at home and have the self-motivation and discipline to see it to completion, choose an online coding bootcamp. You will have access to all the necessary online course materials and instructors, and a large, supportive online student community. Online bootcamp offerings include Dataquest, Datacamp, and Statistics.com.

  • Self-Paced

    The last word in informal, flexible education, the self-paced model enables you to learn and work whenever you feel like it. You get assigned the curriculum, and then you decide how and when you will tackle it. Simplilearn is a good example of a self-paced education provider.
